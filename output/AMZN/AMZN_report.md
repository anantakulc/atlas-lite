## Business

Amazon is a conglomerate built on two compounding platforms (AWS and Advertising), supported by a massive North American retail marketplace and an international operation still building toward profitability. The business earns most of its profit from AWS and Advertising despite those two segments representing only 28% of total revenue, creating a misunderstood value structure where headline P/E and EV/Sales systematically mislead.

**Segments (FY2025 revenue):**
- **AWS:** $128.7bn revenue, 35.4% EBIT margin (Q1 2026: 37.7%) — cloud infrastructure
- **Advertising:** ~$69bn revenue, ~55% contribution margin — retail media, structurally comparable to Meta Family of Apps
- **North America:** $426bn revenue — retail marketplace + subscriptions
- **International:** $162bn revenue — retail outside the US, building toward profitability

## The Three Thesis Pillars

**1. The AWS backlog is the strongest contracted demand signal in cloud history and the valuation does not fully price it.**

The $364bn AWS Remaining Performance Obligation (Q1 2026, company-filed) grew $120bn in a single quarter (+50% QoQ, +93% YoY). At 80-85% conversion over 36 months (Amazon's own disclosed methodology), the existing backlog alone supports $291-309bn of incremental AWS revenue across FY2026-2028 before a single new booking is counted. That contracted floor supports the base revenue path ($162bn FY2026, $196bn FY2027, $230bn FY2028) as a signed commitment, not a projection.

**2. AWS and Advertising are two independently valuable, high-return compounders that together justify the premium.**

AWS: $45.6bn operating income on $128.7bn revenue in FY2025 (35.4% margin, rising to 37.7% in Q1 2026). Advertising: ~$56bn contribution profit on $69bn revenue (55% margin). Together these two segments are worth $160/share of the $212 base, independent of the retail operations. Investors evaluating Amazon on a blended P/E compare a cloud infrastructure and advertising conglomerate to consumer discretionary comps.

**3. The 14.7% pullback from the May 5 all-time high offers a better technical entry within an unchanged fundamental story.**

Amazon printed an all-time high of $278.56 on May 5, 2026 (five trading days post-Q1 results) before retracing 14.7% to $237.50. The pullback was driven by concern over Q1 capex of $44.2bn and near-term FCF compression, not by any change in AWS growth, backlog, or margin trajectory. The stock trades below its 50-day moving average ($256.64) but above its 200-day ($232.71). No fundamental data published since May 5 contradicts the bull thesis.

## Key Debates

### 1. Will the $364bn backlog convert at the implied rate? (THE CRUX)
**Bull:** The backlog's legal status (signed, binding RPO) and the $120bn quarterly addition are the strongest near-term evidence in the bull case. AWS re-accelerated to 28.5% in Q1 2026, the fastest growth in 15 quarters, coincident with the backlog surge.

**Bear:** Backlog conversion depends on customer project timelines, not Amazon's execution alone. Hyperscaler capex commitment is not the same as workload deployment.

**Our verdict:** Contracted. The backlog represents legal obligations with defined conversion schedules. The $364bn at 80% conversion is a floor on revenue, not a ceiling.

**Worth/share swing (bear to bull AWS):** $54.24 (bear) to $101.55 (bull) — $47/share on the AWS lever alone.

---

### 2. FCF compression from $200bn annual capex — temporary or structural?
**Bull:** The 2013-2016 and 2022-2023 cycles both showed that Amazon's capex surges precede, not follow, revenue inflections. Capex peaks when growth is front-loaded; FCF recovers as capacity fills.

**Bear:** $200bn annually is unprecedented. If AWS growth decelerates to 20% before the capacity fills, the depreciation burden ($30bn+ annually) compresses FCF indefinitely without a commensurate revenue payoff.

**Our verdict:** Temporal. The historical precedent holds as long as the backlog converts on schedule (see debate 1). The risk is a timing mismatch, not a structural margin problem.

---

### 3. Is Advertising structurally defensible against Google and TikTok?
**Bull:** Amazon's advertising is product-intent at point of purchase — the highest-value slot in the funnel. Google's search intent is earlier in the journey. The migration of product searches from Google to Amazon is a multi-year trend that does not reverse easily.

**Bear:** Microsoft/Google's generative AI shopping integrations could recapture product-intent searches by inserting Amazon-equivalent purchase recommendations without the click-through. Azure OpenAI is structurally in front of AWS customers.

**Our verdict:** Bear mechanism exists but requires a faster AI-shopping adoption curve than current data supports. The Azure structural competition in cloud is a more immediate risk to AWS than the AI-shopping risk is to Advertising.

---

### 4. Is the $57/share gap between intrinsic convergence ($233) and market target ($290) rational?
**Bull:** The $290 target (28x FY2027E EPS) represents the low end of the post-Q1 analyst cluster (JPMorgan uses 32x). The $364bn backlog justifies a re-rating as it converts to revenue.

**Bear:** If the re-rating takes 18-24 months rather than 12, the stock earns the intrinsic return of -2% to +10% and the BUY is a timing error.

**Our verdict:** We hold the $290 target but assign path_confidence = LOW. The $57 gap is the honest uncertainty in this call. It is not engineered to produce a BUY.

## Bear Paragraph

Amazon's bear case rests on two independent mechanisms. First, $200bn of annual capex and $700bn of five-year data center investment commitments create a depreciation overhang ($30bn+/year) that compresses AWS FCF margins from 37.7% (Q1 2026) toward 33% if revenue growth decelerates to 20%. The bear AWS SOTP is $54/share (NOPAT $59.7bn at 12.15x Gordon, r=11.5%). Second, Azure OpenAI's structural integration into enterprise workflows provides a switching path for AWS workloads that does not require lifting-and-shifting to a new cloud (just switching the AI API endpoint), potentially capping AWS's market share gains at 31-32% of the cloud market. Neither mechanism is a tail risk; both are observable and near-term. The bear SOTP of $145 is the honest lower bound, not a catastrophe scenario.
